    Examination

    Lower limb arterial examination#

    Inspection#

    • Colour (mottling, pallor, necrosis)
    • Ulcers (located at pressure points, small, well defined, punched out edges, deep and painful) – describe site, size, shape, base, surrounding skin.
    • Trophic changes (hair loss, shiny skin)
    • Scars
    • Previous amputation
    • Gangrene

    Palpation#

    • Temperature
    • Capillary refill time
    • Pulses (radial – brachial – carotid – aorta – femoral – popliteal – dorsalis pedis – posterior tibial)
    • Gross sensation

    Special tests#

    • Buerger’s: With the patient lying flat on the bed, elevate the straight legs until the toes become pale. This is Buerger’s angle. Slowly lower the legs over the side of the bed to check for reactive hyperaemia. A Buerger’s angle of less than 20 degrees indicates severe limb ischaemia.

    Complete#

    • Measure BP

    • ABPI: On the left, measure the highest systolic pressure at the dorsalis pedis or posterior tibial artery and divide by the highest brachial systolic pressure. Repeat on right side.

    • Handheld Doppler ultrasound

    • Duplex ultrasound

    Venous examination#

    Inspection#

    • Skin changes: venous eczema, haemosiderin staining, lipodermatosclerosis
    • Ulcers (medial malleolus, shallow, flat, sloped edges, irregular shape)
    • Varicosities: great saphenous down medial side of leg; short saphenous down posterior surface of lower limb; saphena varix (2cm inferior and lateral to pubic tubercle)

    Palpation#

    • Palpate in regions of great saphenous vein, short saphenous vein and saphenofemoral junction

    Special tests#

    • Cough test: place your hand over the saphenofemoral junction and ask the patient to cough. An impulse indicates saphena varix
    • Tap test: place one hand over the saphenofemoral junction and tap the varicosity more distally down the leg. A thrill suggests incompetent valves
    • Tourniquet test: lie the patient down and empty the varicosities. The level at which a tourniquet controls refilling of the vein on standing, indicates the level of the venous incompetence
    • Perthe’s test: ask the patient to stand up on tiptoe, then down, repeatedly with a tourniquet tied around the calf. If varicosities remain large, then this indicates an obstructed deep venous system or reflux in the perforator veins
    • Doppler probe: Place the Doppler probe over the saphenopopliteal junction. On squeezing the calf, a double whoosh indicates reflux of blood in the vein and incompetent valves.

    Complete#

    • Handheld doppler ultrasound
    • Duplex ultrasound
    • ABPI and peripheral arterial examination
