    Superficial Venous Disease

    Background#

    • Varicose veins are dilated and tortuous veins
    • Superficial veins drain into the deep veins through the deep muscle fascia via the perforator veins
    • Valves prevent back flow of blood from deep to superficial veins
    • Varicose veins develop when the superficial vein valves become incompetent, leading to the reflux and pooling of blood in the superficial veins
    • The great saphenous vein, short saphenous vein or perforators may be affected
    • A saphena varix occurs when there is dilatation of the great saphenous vein at the junction with the femoral vein

    Causes:#

    • Primary: usually idiopathic
    • Secondary: intravascular disease (DVT); extravascular disease (pelvic mass)

    Risk factors#

    • Non modifiable: Age; female gender; family history
    • Modifiable: pregnancy, previous DVT, previous trauma to the lower limb

    Presentation#

    • Symptoms: pain (dull ache, heaviness or burning); pain worse after standing and relieved with leg elevation; nocturnal leg cramps; itching
    • Signs: haemosiderin staining; venous eczema; oedema; lipodermatosclerosis; ulceration
    • Complications: ulceration; bleeding; thrombophlebitis; DVT; reduced quality of life

    Diagnosis#

    • Clinical diagnosis
    • Duplex ultrasound can identify location of varicosities and degree of reflux to guide treatment
    • Duplex ultrasound can assess the deep venous system. You need to confirm that the deep system is patent before you can treat and remove the superficial venous system. Either the deep or superficial venous system must be patent to ensure that there is an established route for venous drainage
    • ABPI should be performed to assess for peripheral arterial disease prior to recommendation of compression stockings

    Classification#

    • Classification of varicose veins is by the CEAP classification

    Management#

    • Non surgical: compression stockings; patient education (weight loss, increase exercise to promote calf muscle drainage)
    • Compression stockings need to be worn lifelong and are only recommended if surgical treatment is not appropriate
    • Indications for surgical intervention are: symptomatic varicose veins; recurrent varicose veins; skin changes; ulceration; superficial thrombosis
    • Surgery can be divided into endovenous or open surgery:
      • Endovenous: radiofrequency ablation; injection foam sclerotherapy; glue
      • Open surgery: ligation and stripping; multiple phlebectomies
    • Treatment will often be a combination of techniques
    • Complications of treatment include: bleeding; pain; infection; local nerve damage (sensory changes); recurrence; DVT; skin changes

    Outcome#

    • Recurrent varicose veins occur in up to 20% of patients after treatment

    Deep Venous insufficiency#

    Background:#

    • Deep venous insufficiency is similar to superficial venous insufficiency but affects the deep veins
    • It is a chronic disease caused by a failure of the deep venous system secondary to valve reflux, obstruction or venous hypertension
    • Together, deep and superficial venous disease comprise chronic venous insufficiency

    Causes:#

    • Primary: caused by a defect in the vein wall or valves (connective tissue disease, congenital)
    • Secondary: caused by damage to vessel wall (post thrombotic disease, outflow obstruction, trauma)
    • Individuals with previous DVT may present with Post Thrombotic Syndrome, especially following ilio-femoral DVT

    Risk factors#

    • Non modifiable: age; female gender; family history
    • Modifiable: pregnancy; previous DVT; smoking; obesity; prolonged standing

    Presentation#

    • Signs: lower limb swelling; venous ulcers; oedema; evidence of previous DVT (venous eczema, haemosiderin staining, lipodermatosclerosis, atrophie blanche)
    • Symptoms: aching pain; itching; venous claudication
    • Complications: swelling; recurrent cellulitis; ulceration; chronic pain

    Diagnosis#

    • Diagnosis is by duplex ultrasound which can assess for reflux, vessel wall changes and DVT
    • ABPI should be performed to assess for peripheral arterial disease prior to recommendation of compression stockings
    • A venogram can give further information regarding occlusion/reflux

    Management#

    • Non surgical: lifestyle measures (weight loss, smoking cessation); compression stockings
    • Surgical treatment is limited. Individuals may benefit from deep venous stenting or bypass in some specialised centres
