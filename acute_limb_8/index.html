    Acute limb ischaemia

    Background#

    • Acute limb ischaemia is a sudden decrease in arterial perfusion of a limb which threatens it viability
    • The limb can survive for between 4-6 hours before irreversible tissue damage
    • Acute on chronic limb ischaemia is usually secondary to thrombosis in patients with pre-existing arterial disease where atherosclerotic plaque rupture leads to lumen reduction and thrombosis

    Causes#

    • Causes can be divided into embolic and thrombotic
    • Thrombotic causes account for 80-85% of cases
    • Thrombotic causes include: atherosclerosis; low flow (congestive cardiac failure; hypovolaemia); arteritis; external compression; dissection; hypercoagulation, malignancy
    • Embolic causes include: atherosclerotic plaque or thrombus from the heart, more proximal arteries or aortic/peripheral aneurysms; valvular heart disease (rheumatic, bacterial); iatrogenic; trauma; paradoxical emboli

    Risk factors:#

    • Non modifiable: age
    • Modifiable: hypertension; hyperlipidaemia; smoking; diabetes; obesity; cancer

    Presentation#

    • Acute limb ischaemia is characterised by the 6 P’s: pain; pallor; paraesthesia; paralysis; pulselessness; perishing cold.
    • Skin changes, including, ulcers, shiny skin, loss of hair, are associated with chronic limb ischaemia.
    • Nerves are affected first by ischaemia, followed by muscle then skin. Muscle wasting and paralysis are late signs of ischaemia.

    Diagnosis#

    • Hand-held Doppler can be used at the bedside to assess pulses
    • Creatinine kinase (CK) can be used to assess severity of muscle ischaemia
    • Baseline bloods, and group and save
    • CT angiography is used for diagnosis

    Management#

    • Start patient on systemic anticoagulation with IV heparin infusion and IV fluid rehydration
    • Treatment can be endovascular or surgical depending on stage of ischaemia
    • Endovascular treatments can be used for acute on chronic limb ischaemia and include: catheter-directed thrombolysis; stenting; suction thrombectomy, mechanical thrombectomy
    • Surgical treatments include: thrombo-embolectomy; bypass; amputation
    • Post procedure, all patients should: commence antiplatelet therapy; may require ongoing anticoagulation, stop smoking; start a high dose statin; optimise management of hypertension and diabetes; receive diet and exercise advice, investigate and treat underlying cause.

    Outcome#

    • Acute limb ischaemia has a mortality rate of 10-15%
    • Complications arise from the metabolic effects of reperfusion
    • Reperfusion injury includes: compartment syndrome; hyperkalaemia; acidosis and AKI
    • Restoring blood flow to damaged muscle tissue can cause further ischaemic cell damage. Rhabdomyolysis results in the release of toxic metabolites, including K+ and myoglobin, into the circulation. The release of these toxic metabolites can cause AKI, and its associated complications including acidaemia, uraemia, pulmonary oedema and death
