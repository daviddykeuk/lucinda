    Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm

    Background#

    • Definition: an abdominal aortic aneurysm is an abnormal dilatation of the abdominal aorta so that its diameter is >3cm or 50% greater than the expected diameter

    Causes#

    • Inflammation secondary to atherosclerosis, hypertension, infection, trauma, arteritis
    • Connective tissue disorders

    Risk factors#

    • Non modifiable: male gender; age; family history; connective tissue disorders (Marfans, Ehlers Danlos)
    • Modifiable: smoking; hypertension; hyperlipidaemia

    Screening#

    • Men over 65
    • Abdominal duplex ultrasound
    • Below indicates the indications for referral and consideration of intervention. Individuals should also be referred to the local vascular service if growth of 0.5cm over 6 months
    Size in cmPlan
    < 3Discharge from screening
    3 – 4.4 (small AA)Annual surveillance scans
    4.5 – 5.4 (medium AAA)3 monthly surveillance scans
    >5.5 (large AAA)Refer to local vascular service

    Presentation#

    • Usually asymptomatic
    • May be identified on screening or incidentally on imaging for other conditions
    • May cause symptoms from: local compression; embolization; fistulisation
    • Symptomatic presentation at rupture: abdominal/back pain; hypotension

    Investigations#

    • Abdominal duplex ultrasound in screening
    • FAST scan can be performed in A&E however is not used to diagnose a AAA
    • CT angiography is 100% sensitive for an aneurysm and is used for diagnosis and surgical planning

    Management#

    Elective#

    • Mange cardiovascular risk factors: weight loss; smoking cessation; blood pressure control; statin; antiplatelet
    • Surgical repair is considered for: AAA >5.5cm or growth >1cm/year or a symptomatic aneurysm
    • Can be managed by open or endovascular repair

    Emergency#

    • Resuscitation including cross match for 6 units of blood
    • Resuscitate with “permissive hypotension”. Raising the blood pressure may dislodge any precipitated clot and cause further bleeding
    • Most patients will receive a CT angiography prior to intervention both for diagnosis and surgical planning if the patient is stable enough for transfer to CT
    • If the patient is too unstable for CT, then they may require immediate transfer to theatre
    • Can be managed by open or endovascular repair
