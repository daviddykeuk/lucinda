    Chronic Limb Ischaemia

    Background#

    • Chronic limb ischaemia is a symptomatic reduced arterial blood supply to the limbs

    Causes#

    • Primary: vasculitis
    • Secondary: atherosclerosis; radiation; trauma; inflammation secondary to infection; rare causes such as popliteal artery entrapment

    Risk Factors#

    • Non modifiable: age; family history
    • Modifiable: smoking; T2DM; hypertension; obesity; hyperlipidaemia

    Presentation#

    • Presentation depends on the severity of disease
    • Individuals may present with claudication, a muscular, cramp like pain when walking, which is relieved by rest. They may also report night pain, eased by hanging the leg out of the bed or sleeping in a chair.
    • Claudication can occur in the buttock, thigh, calf or foot. The location of pain indicates the level of arterial occlusion
    • On examination individuals may have absent foot pulses, ulceration or gangrene. You would expect to find a prolonged capillary refill time, cool extremities, muscle atrophy, and skin changes (thin, shiny skin, hair loss and dystrophic nail changes)
    • Leriche syndrome is disease of the aortic bifurcation. It presents as a triad of: buttock/thigh claudication; erectile dysfunction; reduced femoral pulses
    • Classification of chronic limb ischaemia is by the Fontaine or Rutherford classification

    Fontaine Classification#

    StageClinical feature
    IAsymptomatic
    IIaMild claudication
    IIbModerate – severe claudication
    IIIRest pain
    IVUlceration or gangrene

    Rutherford Classification#

    StageClinical feature
    0Asymptomatic
    1Mild claudication
    2Moderate claudication
    3Severe claudication
    4Rest pain
    5Minor tissue loss
    6Severe tissue loss extending above the transmetatarsal level and foot no longer salvageable

    Critical Limb Ischaemia#

    • Critical limb ischaemia is advanced chronic limb ischaemia (Fontaine III/IV or Rutherford 4-6)
    • It is defined as one of the following:
      • Rest pain for more than two weeks
      • Ulceration or gangrene
      • ABPI < 0.4, ankle pressue <50mmHg, toe pressue <30mmHg

    Investigations#

    • Chronic limb ischaemia is a clinical diagnosis
    • ABPI is used to confirm the diagnosis and classify severity
    SeverityABPI
    Normal> 0.9 < 1.2
    Mild0.8 – 0.9
    Moderate0.5 - 0.8
    Severe/critical ischaemia< 0.4
    • Duplex ultrasound is performed after ABPI to assess the location and severity of disease
    • CT/MR angiography can be used to further characterise location and extent of disease
    • Digital subtraction angiography can be used to assess disease, and may also provide treatment by angioplasty or stenting
    • Individuals may also receive an ECHO prior to intervention due to their cardiovascular risks

    Management#

    • Non surgical: risk factor modification (smoking cessation, weight loss, high dose statin, anti platelet, diabetic control); supervised exercise programme
    • Surgical intervention should be offered if lifestyle modification and supervised exercise fail to improve symptoms
    • Surgical: angioplasty (+/- stenting); endarterectomy; bypass graft; amputation

    Outcomes#

    • Complications of chronic limb ischaemia include: acute on chronic ischaemia; sepsis secondary to gangrene; reduced quality of life
    • In individuals with intermittent claudication, most have stable disease. 10-20% have worsening symptoms and 5-10% develop critical limb ischaemia.
    • 5 year mortality in critical limb ischaemia is 50%
