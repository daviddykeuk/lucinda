    Vascular Investigations

    Doppler#

    Principles#

    • Uses the change in frequency of sound waves reflected off a moving object
    • Used to determine the velocity of blood
    • Triphasic waveform represents a healthy vessel. Biphasic and monophasic represent a diseased vessel wall.
    • Triphasic:
      • Phase 1. In systole, blood accelerates forwards
      • Phase 2. At the end of systole, a drop in pressure leads to reverse flow in the vessel
      • Phase 3. Elastic recoil in the vessel wall leads to forward flow in the vessel
    • Biphasic:
      • Occurs when vessels lose their elasticity
      • Loss of phase 3
    • Monophasic:
      • One sound produced
      • Represents vessel damage
      • Loss of phases 2 and 3

    Indications#

    • Assessment of pulses
    • Calculate ABPI

    Advantages#

    • Non invasive
    • Quick and convenient

    Limitations#

    • Limited information
    • You can never find a hand held Doppler on the ward!

    Duplex Ultrasound#

    Principles#

    • Uses high frequency sound waves
    • Duplex (“dual”) combines traditional ultrasound with doppler
      • Traditional ultrasound produces a greyscale picture of the area (structure of the vessel)
      • Doppler assesses flow in the blood vessel (assessment of stenosis)

    Indications#

    • Diagnosis of: DVT; venous insufficiency; cerebrovascular disease; renal disease; mesenteric disease; aortoiliac disease
    • First line investigation for chronic arterial disease

    Advantages#

    • Non-invasive, mobile, well tolerated
    • Comprehensive
    • Does not involve ionising radiation or contrast

    Limitations#

    • Operator dependent
    • Limited by body habitus
    • Calcium does not transmit sound waves making assessment of calcified arteries difficult
    • Gas in the bowel slows transmission of sound waves making assessment of intraabdominal vessels difficult

    CT angiography#

    Principles:#

    • Combines traditional CT with injection of contrast dye

    Indications:#

    • Can assess any part of the arterial circulation
    • Used for assessment and to plan for repair of aortic aneurysms

    Advantages#

    • Non-invasive and comprehensive

    Limitations#

    • High doses ionising radiation
    • Contrast required- nephrotoxic

    MR angiography#

    Principles#

    • Combines traditional MRI with injection of contrast dye

    Indications#

    • Assess any part of the arterial circulation
    • Assessment of soft tissue tumours and AVM

    Advantages#

    • Non invasive and comprehensive
    • No ionising radiation

    Limitations#

    • Contraindicated in patients with pacemakers
    • Contrast required
    • Expensive

    Digital subtraction angiography (DSA)#

    Principles:#

    • Digital fluroscopy (xray) captures images before and after injection of intra arterial contrast. The pre contrast image is subtracted from the post contrast image to give an image of the contrast filled arteries

    Indications#

    • Assessment and treatment of arterial pathology

    Advantages#

    • Gold standard investigation of arterial disease
    • Can be diagnostic and therapeutic (angioplasty, stenting)

    Limitations#

    • High doses ionising radiation
    • Contrast required
    • Requires intra-arterial puncture
    • Patient must be able to lie flat during procedure and for 4 hours afterward
    • Associated risks: bleeding, haematoma, false aneurysms, embolisation, damage to vessel wall (dissection or rupture)
