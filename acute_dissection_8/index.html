    Acute Type B dissection

    Definitions#

    • Acute aortic syndrome includes aortic dissection, intra-mural haematoma (IMH) and penetrating aortic ulcer (PAU), which are a spectrum of conditions with similar presentation and management
    • Aortic dissection occurs when there is a breach in the intima (inner layer of the vessel wall) and blood tracks between the intima and media, leading to a true and false lumen
    • An aneurysm is a 50% increase in the normal diameter of a vessel. It involves all three layers of the vessel wall
    • A false aneurysm occurs when there is a breach in the vessel wall, which blood tracks through. The blood is then contained by the adventitia or surrounding perivascular soft tissue
    • Stanford Type A aortic dissection affects the ascending aorta, and may propagate to the arch or descending aorta
    • Stanford Type B aortic dissection affects the descending thoracic aorta, with the entry tear distal to the left subclavian artery

    Presentation#

    • Chest pain and shortness of breath
    • “Tearing” or “ripping” type pain which can spread to the back, neck or jaw
    • Syncope
    • Focal neurology: visual changes; weakness
    • Bowel ischaemia: abdominal pain; loose stool; blood in stool
    • Limb ischaemia: 6 Ps – pale, painful, pallor, pulseless, paraesthesia, perishingly cold, paralysis
    • Renal ischaemia: Renal infarction, worsening renal function, decreased urine output, acute kidney injury

    Risk Factors#

    • Most common in men aged 60-80
    • Non modifiable: Congenital aortic anomalies (bicuspid aortic valves, aortic coarctation); family history; connective tissue disorders (Marfan syndrome, Ehlers- Danlos syndrome)
    • Modifiable: hypertension; atherosclerosis; smoking; pregnancy; cocaine use

    Diagnosis#

    • CT angiogram of the aorta
    • Perform ECG, CXR, ECHO, and baseline bloods including troponin and lactate as part of work up

    Management#

    • Stanford type A dissections are treated with urgent open surgical repair under cardiothoracics
    • Uncomplicated Stanford type B dissections are treated medically with close monitoring and aggressive blood pressure management with intravenous beta blockers such as labetalol in the acute setting
    • Once BP is stabilised, patients will require lifelong antihypertensive therapy and surveillance imaging to monitor for aneurysmal dilatation
    • Indication for surgical intervention for type B dissections includes: end organ ischaemia; aortic rupture; significant or rapid aneurysmal dilatation; persistent pain; uncontrolled hypertension
    • Surgical intervention may be open or endovascular or hybrid (combined open and endovascular)

    Outcome#

    • Mortality of aortic dissection is high – 20% patients die before reaching hospital
    • Early diagnosis and BP control improves prognosis
    • Complications in the acute setting include: aortic rupture; aortic aneurysm; stroke; myocardial ischaemia, cardiac tamponade, limb ischaemia, renal ischaemia, mesenteric ischaemia
