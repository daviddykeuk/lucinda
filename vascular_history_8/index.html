    Vascular history

    Summary#

    Presenting complaint#

    Pain: SOCRATES#

    • Aneurysm: Usually asymptomatic but patients may report abdominal or back pain
    • Peripheral arterial disease: A cramping type pain in the buttock or calf which occurs with exertion, usually at a consistent distance. The pain is relieved by rest. Ask how far the patient can walk before they have pain/need to stop (claudication distance). In critical limb ischaemia, individuals describe rest pain or night pain, which is relieved by hanging the foot over the edge of the bed. Individuals may sleep upright in a chair. In critical limb ischaemia, patients may have ulcers.
    • Peripheral venous: A heavy, aching pain in the legs, with associated leg swelling and tightness. Pain is worse when standing and relieved by leg elevation. Individuals may describe itching, skin changes, varicose veins, and ulcers.
    • Neurogenic claudication: May occur with spinal stenosis. Neurogenic claudication is leg pain on exertion which is worse with spinal extension and relieved with spine flexion. Pulses should be normal unless concurrent vascular disease
    • Assess impact of symptoms on quality of life

    Past medical history#

    • Vasculopaths may have a history of previous MI or stroke
    • Risk factors for arterial disease: T2DM; hypertension; hyperlipidaemia; obesity; hypercoagulable state
    • Risk factors for venous disease: previous DVT, previous lower limb trauma, previous pregnancy

    Medications history#

    • Is the patient on secondary prevention for arterial disease: statin, antiplatelet, anti-hypertensive (if indicated), diabetic medication (if indicated)
    • Patient analgesic requirements

    Family history#

    • Both a risk factor for aortic aneurysm and superficial venous disease

    Social history#

    • Smoking history
    • Diet and exercise
    • Alcohol intake
    • Previous occupation (prolonged standing as a risk factor for superficial venous disease)
    • Impact of disease on mobility and ADLs
